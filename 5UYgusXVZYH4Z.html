Tips on how to prevent testicular cancer

Testicular cancer is a relatively rare disease, but it affects men of reproductive age from 15 to 50 years, so its prevention is especially important. Clear risk factors or completely effective methods prevent this disease in men does not exist. The following tips are more likely to help detect the disease at an early stage and, therefore, avoid its complications.

What can you do to reduce your risk of testicular cancer?

Check your testicles regularly. Most cases of testicular cancer are detected by chance. To reduce the risk of late-stage cancer detection, you need a self-examination. It is better to do this after a bath, since as a result of heating, the skin of the scrotum covering the testicles relaxes, and it is easier to feel lumps or changes in the consistency of the testicles. Examine the testicles with both hands, it is important to examine each testicle in turn separately. Carefully look for any changes or differences in the size of the two testicles.

Men who have had testicular malformations in the past are at an increased risk of developing cancer in this area and should know how to properly examine their testicles. Such risk conditions are various anomalies (improper testicular development, etc.). If there have already been cases of testicular cancer in the family, the risk also increases.

It is important to visit a urologist at least once a year to examine the testicles and monitor any worrying symptoms.

What are the most common symptoms of testicular cancer?

- feeling seals in one of the testicles;

- the difference in the size of two testicles - one looks much larger than the other;

- dull pain in the lower abdomen or groin;

- sudden accumulation of fluid in the scrotum;

- feeling of heaviness in the testicles;

- swelling in this area.

If you have any of the following for these symptoms, it is recommended that you consult a doctor who will assess your condition and, if necessary, suggest appropriate treatment.
